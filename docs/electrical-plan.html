01 Power Budget & The Stator Caveat

Everything downstream is constrained by how much current the bike can spare. Get this wrong and you cook a stator — a known weak point on this generation.

~400 W
Total alternator @ 5000 rpm
~100–120 W
Realistic accessory surplus
~8–9 A
Usable headroom @ 13.8 V cruise
≈ 0 W
Surplus at idle / low rpm
Critical — shunt regulator

The DL650 uses a shunt-type regulator/rectifier: the stator always produces near-maximum output and the R/R dumps the excess to ground as heat. Reducing your draw does not relieve the stator. This generation had stator/R-R heat issues. Two moves matter:

  • Convert the headlight to LED — frees roughly 50 W of real capacity for accessories.
  • Consider a series R/R upgrade (e.g. a Shindengen MOSFET unit) if you'll routinely run heated grips + aux lights together. It draws only what's needed and runs the stator cooler.

Design principle: build the harness with more capacity than the charging system can sustain. The wiring should never be the bottleneck. What you actively switch on at once is what you manage — the firmware can even read bus voltage and shed load (dim lights, cut grips) when voltage sags at idle. That's a feature, not a limitation, and it's why the ESP measures voltage anyway.

02 Engine-Running Logic — the heart of the system

You want accessories live only when the engine runs, a customizable off-delay after key-off, and immunity to a battery tender. One ignition wire plus a voltage reading does all three.

The rule

engineRunning = ignitionHIGH && (Vbus ≥ 13.2 V)

Ignition alone can't separate "running" from "key-on, engine-off." Voltage alone gets fooled by a charger. Together they're unambiguous.

Truth table — every state the bike can be in

Why the ESP must be always-hot

Because the off-delay runs after the key is off, the ESP can't be powered by the switched line — it would die the instant you needed it to keep counting. It runs from a small always-hot fused buck, deep-sleeps at ~10 µA–2 mA when parked (negligible drain over weeks), and wakes on the ignition wire's rising edge. The ignition wire therefore does triple duty: wake source, run-detect, and off-delay trigger.

05 ESP32 Power, Protection & Sense

Automotive 12V is hostile — load-dump spikes, cranking sag, reverse-polarity risk during service. The input stage matters more than the ESP itself.

CP2102 boards — programming vs install

Your USB-C CP2102 boards: in the installed bike harness, power the board from the buck's 5V pin and treat USB-C as a programming-only port. Don't drive 5V into both VIN and USB simultaneously. Keep the USB port reachable (a short pigtail to a capped, weather-sealed access point) so you can reflash without pulling the bay apart — a small service-loop courtesy to your future self.

06 Per-Load MOSFET & PWM Dimming

Downstream of the master bus, each controllable load gets a logic-level N-channel MOSFET on its low side. On/off plus PWM dimming, all from ESP GPIOs.

v1 reality — this section is the v2 upgrade

The fuse/relay box you ordered uses mechanical relays, so v1 switches fog and grips ON/OFF only — the ESP energises each load's relay coil through a small coil driver (logic-level transistor/FET + flyback diode across the coil), and the GPIO acts as a simple enable (FOG_EN / GRIP_EN), not a PWM output. No dimming yet, which is fine. The MOSFET scheme above is the v2 drop-in: replace a load's relay with a logic-level MOSFET on the same enable line and you gain PWM dimming without rewiring — only the switching element changes. Plan the GPIO and wiring now; swap the part later.

07 Grounding Backbone — Star Topology

Clean grounds are why your ADC readings and BLE behave. Every accessory ground returns to one bus, the bus returns to the battery negative on one heavy conductor. No daisy-chaining, no random frame grounds.

Connector & service discipline

  • One main front↔rear disconnect at the headstock — Deutsch DT or sealed automotive multi-pin. Everything forward unplugs here.
  • Key power and signal differently — use different connector bodies or sizes so a 12V line can never mate into a signal pin.
  • Service loops: leave 10–15 cm of slack at every termination and at the disconnect, so steering travel never tugs a pin.
  • Label both ends of every conductor (heat-shrink labels). Future-you will not remember which grey wire is the spare.
  • Run two spares populated-but-capped — the entire point of doing this once.
  • BLE means data doesn't go forward — the rotary controller and tablet talk wirelessly, so the spine carries power and a couple of just-in-case signal lines, nothing more.

10 Tablet Recommendation

For a permanent mount, heat and battery swelling are the real enemies — not specs. One Samsung feature decisively solves that.

Top pick — Galaxy Tab Active5 (or Active5 Pro)

The killer feature is No Battery Mode: the tablet runs with the battery removed, on continuous external power, specifically so the battery doesn't sit in a hot enclosure and swell. Samsung markets this exactly for "kiosks and vehicles." For a bike that bakes in the sun, that's the difference between a 5-year install and a puffy battery in two summers.

  • Caveats: No Battery Mode needs a dedicated USB-C PD 2.0+ source (≈21 W / 9V·2.3A for the Pro) and it limits CPU, brightness, and volume while battery-less. Size your tablet PD buck accordingly (hence ≥21 W in §08).
  • Active5 = 8″, Gorilla Glass 5; Active5 Pro = 10.1″, 120 Hz, 600 nits, dual hot-swap batteries, 8 yrs of updates.
  • Both: IP68, MIL-STD-810H, 2 mm-glove touch, wet-touch, programmable key (map it to your dashboard app), POGO-pin charging for a clean dock.

Budget alternative: the Tab Active3 (and Active4 Pro) also support No Battery Mode and glove touch — a good used-market value if you can live with older silicon and a dimmer screen. Your first-gen Tab Active is genuinely fine for bench prototyping the BLE/UI, just not for the permanent outdoor mount. Install tips regardless of model: mount it in No-Battery Mode powered only when the engine runs (your switched line handles this), use the POGO dock rather than a fragile USB-C plug for the permanent connection, and map the programmable key to wake/launch your dashboard.

12 Install Sequence — Do It Once

Fairings come off once. Run and terminate everything, including the spares, before they go back on.

01Disconnect battery negative first. Always. Confirm with a meter.
02Mount the under-seat tray: ground bus, master relay + socket, fuse block, sealed ESP enclosure. Plan layout dry before crimping.
03With fairings off, run the spine front→rear: tablet power pair, front-aux pair, spare pair, shielded signal pair. Secure clear of heat, steering, and pinch points.
04Set service loops (10–15 cm) at both ends; fit the main disconnect at the headstock. Label every conductor at both ends now.
05Install the main fuse within 15 cm of battery +. Run 12 AWG to the relay 30-pin and to the distribution block.
06Build the ESP input stage on the always-hot 2 A tap: reverse-pol → TVS → bulk cap → buck. Set and verify the buck to 5.0 V on the bench before connecting the ESP.
07Wire the ignition-sense Add-A-Fuse → divider/Zener → ESP wake GPIO. Wire INA226 across the bus (I²C to ESP).
08Wire the relay coil: 85→+12, 86→ESP MOSFET (low-side), flyback diode across coil. 87→accessory bus.
09Land per-load MOSFETs (gate resistor + 10 k pulldown), sources to the ground bus. Fuse each load on the switched bus.
10Land all grounds to the star bus; single 12 AWG return to battery −. No daisy-chains, no frame grounds.
11Reconnect battery. Bench-test the state machine with a variable supply: simulate 12.5 V (off), 12.5 V + ign (armed), 13.5 V + ign (run), ign-off (delay), charger case (13.8 V no ign → stays off).
12Only after the logic passes, refit fairings. Leave the USB programming pigtail accessible and sealed.

13 Firmware State Logic

The supervisor loop, in plain pseudocode. The ignition key is the master switch — once powered, accessories stay on until the key goes OFF; engine-run (bus voltage) only gates the first power-on and tells RUNNING (charging) apart from POWERED (engine off, key on). Hysteresis + debounce avoid chatter. Full rationale in ADR-0012.

// ---- tunable parameters (tablet-configurable over BLE) ----
const V_RUN_ON      = 13.2;    // engine confirmed running
const V_RUN_OFF     = 12.9;    // below = not charging (engine stopped)
const RUN_DEBOUNCE  = 1000;    // ms above V_RUN_ON before RUNNING
const STALL_DEBOUNCE = 2000;   // ms below V_RUN_OFF before POWERED
const OFF_DELAY     = 30000;   // ms relay held after KEY-OFF
const BACKSTOP      = 600000;  // ms in POWERED before battery-save cut (10 min)
const CHARGE_WARN   = [12.8, 13.4);  // V band: marginal-charging warning

void supervisorLoop() {
  ign  = digitalRead(IGN_SENSE);        // HIGH only with key on
  vbus = readBusVoltage();              // from INA3221, not raw ADC

  switch (state) {

    case SLEEP:                          // woke on ignition edge (ext0)
      if (ign) state = ARMED;
      else     enterDeepSleep(WAKE_ON_IGN);
      break;

    case ARMED:                          // key on, engine not running; relay OFF
      if (!ign) { state = SLEEP; break; }
      if (vbus >= V_RUN_ON for RUN_DEBOUNCE) {
        setRelay(ON);  state = RUNNING;   // engine confirmed
      }
      break;

    case RUNNING:                        // key on, engine running; relay ON
      if (!ign) { startTimer(OFF_DELAY); state = OFF_DELAY_HOLD; break; }
      if (vbus < V_RUN_OFF for STALL_DEBOUNCE) {
        state = POWERED;                 // stall/kill, key on — relay STAYS on
      } else if (vbus in CHARGE_WARN) {
        warn("charging marginal — loads high or R/R weak");
        manageLoads(vbus);              // (future) shed grips -> fog -> ...
      }
      break;

    case POWERED:                        // key on, engine OFF; relay STILL ON
      if (!ign) { startTimer(OFF_DELAY); state = OFF_DELAY_HOLD; break; }
      if (vbus >= V_RUN_ON for RUN_DEBOUNCE) { state = RUNNING; break; }  // restarted
      if (elapsed(POWERED) >= BACKSTOP) {
        setRelay(OFF);  state = ARMED;  // forgot the key — save battery
      }
      break;

    case OFF_DELAY_HOLD:                 // key off, relay ON, counting down
      if (ign) { state = (vbus >= V_RUN_ON) ? RUNNING : POWERED; break; } // key back
      if (timerExpired()) {
        setRelay(OFF);  state = SLEEP;     // then deep sleep
      }
      break;
  }
}

// CHARGER CASE: key off => ign LOW => only ever SLEEP, however high a tender
// pushes vbus. A stall/kill keeps power (POWERED); a forgotten key is caught
// by the BACKSTOP. Solved.

14 Sensing & Multi-Wake Architecture

The same always-on ESP serves two lives: cockpit controller when you ride, parked sentinel the rest of the week. What separates them is which event woke it. Deep sleep is the default state; wake sources branch the behaviour.

Sensor choice

Use a 6-axis IMU (accel + gyro) — LSM6DSO, ICM-20948, or the budget MPU6050 — not a bare accelerometer. The gyro is what tells a real get-off (bike rotating, going horizontal) from a bump, and these parts have a hardware wake-on-motion interrupt that runs at microamps, so one chip covers theft-wake, crash-detect, and motion logging. Wire it on the same I²C bus as the INA226.

15 Crash Detection — Built Around False Positives

The hard part isn't sensing a big jolt — it's not crying wolf. A system that texts your emergency contact every time the bike tips in the garage gets switched off within a week. The whole design is a filter plus a cancel window.

Honest limitation — read this

A homebrew crash-SOS is a helpful augmentation, not a guaranteed safety device. It can miss a real crash (false negative) as well as fire on a non-crash. Build it and enjoy it, but don't let it replace any other safety habit, and don't depend on it the way you would a certified eCall system.

16 Battery Telemetry & Alert Logic

The INA226 already in the plan gives this for free. The insight: the rate of voltage change separates your two real scenarios — "I left something on" from "it's just been sitting." Track the trend, not a single number.

Why the morning check is the right time

Resting voltage only reflects true state-of-charge after the battery has settled — surface charge from a ride or charger has to dissipate (a few hours). Your 24 h timer firing in the early morning, after an overnight sit, samples exactly when the reading is trustworthy. So the daily wake does triple duty: GPS position (did it move?), resting-voltage health, and IMU self-test — one short wake, three answers, back to sleep.

Sense where there's no current — and folding in the panel voltmeter

A voltmeter shows the voltage at its tap point, not at the battery. Tap into a wire that also feeds loads — an ignition-switched accessory wire, say — and you read battery voltage minus the IR drop across the ignition switch, its connectors, and that shared wire. On an older bike that drop is easily ~1 V under load, which is exactly the gap a panel meter spliced into such a wire will show versus a meter on the battery posts. The meter isn't faulty; it's faithfully reporting a lossy point. The rule: never sense voltage on a current-carrying wire. Read it on a dedicated sense lead that carries only the meter/ADC's microamps — zero current, zero drop, true voltage. This is the same battery-+ sense reference the R/R sense lead (§23) and the INA226 use.

So the system already is the accurate voltmeter: the INA226 sits on a clean sense point and the tablet shows the number — and it shows more than a dumb meter can (settled resting V, live charging V, and the rate-of-change of §16). Treat the tablet readout as the real gauge. If a physical panel meter stays on the bars, wire it correctly: its + lead from a dedicated sense wire off the battery + (or the R/R output to watch the regulator directly), gated to power up only when the engine runs — a small relay/transistor on the engine-run signal, or the master-gated feed tapped right at the battery side of the master relay — and its − to the star ground. Because the meter draws almost nothing, that lead has no measurable drop, so it reads the true 14.2–14.4 V when charging instead of 13-point-something. The one thing to avoid is re-splicing it onto any load wire — that's what put it 1 V down to begin with.

17 Connectivity Model — Matched to Your Garage

A shared underground garage means no WiFi and no public position to phone home from. But you and your wife walk past the bike on the way to the car regularly — so the bike doesn't need its own radio in v1. Your phones are the radio.

Design honestly for two things
  • Background BLE is fussy. Android needs a foreground service to scan reliably; iOS restricts background BLE harder. Expect to tune reconnection, and consider a "just opened the app" manual sync as the dependable fallback. Pair both your phone and your wife's so either pass-by syncs.
  • The relay is not real-time theft alerting. A thief won't carry the bike past your phone. It nails battery health and in-garage tamper; genuine drive-away theft is the v2-LTE case. Knowing that boundary is what lets you defer LTE without a false sense of cover.

18 Sentinel-Mode State Machine

The supervisor from §13 gains a branch: on wake, the source decides cockpit vs. sentinel. Sentinel work is short — sample, decide, queue, sleep.

void onWake(wakeCause) {
  switch (wakeCause) {

    case WAKE_IGNITION:                 // key turned on
      enterCockpitMode();             // → supervisorLoop() from §13
      break;

    case WAKE_MOTION:                   // IMU interrupt while parked
      imu = readIMU();
      if (looksLikeCrash(imu)) runCrashCountdown();   // §15
      else if (displacedFromParked()) queue("bike moved");
      break;

    case WAKE_TIMER:                    // daily ~AM check-in
      v = restingVoltage();            // settled → trustworthy
      queue(batteryReport(v));        // trend + threshold → §16
      queue(positionReport());        // moved since last sync?
      break;
  }
  trySyncOverBLE(timeout=8s);          // a paired phone in range? drain queue
  enterDeepSleep(WAKE_IGN | WAKE_MOTION | WAKE_TIMER_24H);
}

// v2 adds: case WAKE_POWERLOSS -> immediate LTE alert from backup LiPo.

19 v1 / v2 Build Roadmap

Phase by value-per-effort. v1 uses parts you have or that cost a few dollars and covers your everyday risks; v2 is the radio revision for the rare drive-away case. Crucially, v1 reserves the bay space and a spine pair for v2 so the fairings stay on.

v1 — sensors + relay (now)

  • 6-axis IMU on the I²C bus (motion wake, crash, tilt)
  • INA226 battery telemetry + trend logic
  • Multi-wake: ignition + motion + 24 h timer
  • Crash detection with 30 s cancel window
  • Crash SOS via tablet (riding) — uses tablet's own LTE
  • BLE companion app on both phones = opportunistic relay
  • Battery-health + "did it move" alerts on pass-by sync
  • Reserve: a spine pair, antenna routing, and bay volume for v2

v2 — the radio revision (later)

  • LTE Cat-M / NB-IoT modem (e.g. SIM7000-class) + data SIM
  • GPS module (u-blox NEO-M8N/M9N) + antenna
  • Small backup LiPo + charger (survives battery cut)
  • Power-loss detection → immediate theft alert
  • Low-voltage cutoff (preserve cranking charge ~11.8 V)
  • Standalone drive-away tracking — the gap v1 can't see
  • Event-driven only (motion/heartbeat) to last weeks parked

21 Hardware Integration — As-Built Decisions

Reflecting the actual parts: a pre-wired 4-relay / 6-fuse sealed box (4× JD1912 40A relays), left stock, with the brains in their own enclosure beside it.

Two enclosures, not one

The relay box is ~100 × 70 × 44 mm; with ~28 mm relays inside there isn't room to lid-mount the ESP. So the relay box stays stock and sealed, and the ESP + buck + IMU + INA226 live in their own small sealed enclosure mounted beside it in the bay. This is also better electrically — it keeps the switching relays physically away from the sensitive electronics and the short I²C bus. The only wires between the two boxes are the relay coil-control leads, a shared ground, and the power feed. The IMU goes inside the ESP box on short I²C; hard-mount the box and calibrate its resting orientation in firmware (the whole bike moves as one body, so location doesn't matter — rigidity and calibration do). The INA226 only needs bus voltage here, which it can read anywhere on +12 V, so it sits happily in the ESP box too. (Alternative if you prefer one unit: a 3D-printed taller lid in ASA/PETG — not PLA, it softens under-seat — with a gasket channel and a wire gland.)

The combo box, confirmed

The box is a 4-relay / 6-fuse unit (4× JD1912 40 A relays). On inspection: four fuses protect the four relay outputs, and two fuses (the 5 A and the 30 A) feed straight out, bypassing the relays. So those two are "dumb" direct outputs. The move is to feed the box's main 12 V through an external master relay the ESP energises only when the engine is running — that hardware-gates the whole box, and as a bonus turns the 5 A and 30 A direct-outs into engine-switched 12 V feeds (e.g. tablet buck on the 5 A, a switched sub-bus on the 30 A). The master relay doing the engine-gate in hardware means the four internal relays just need plain user on/off.

ESP power — its own battery tap, not the box

Because the master relay now gates the box, the ESP cannot draw its always-hot supply from inside the box (it would die the moment the master relay opens, killing the off-delay and parked sentinel). Run the ESP's supply from a separate always-hot tap straight off the battery, with an inline 2 A fuse, upstream of the master relay. The ESP controls the master relay; it can't live downstream of it.

Relay coil drive — ULN2803, reusing the commoned coil pin

The box's four coil "negatives" (pin 86, black) come out commoned. Rather than separate them, tie that common to +12 V (the master-gated feed) and switch each individual pin 85 (white) to ground — coils aren't polarised, so swapping +/- is fine. Drive them with a ULN2803A Darlington array: it's an open-collector low-side sink (exactly this topology), its inputs run from 3.3 V logic, and it's active-high (GPIO high → output sinks the 85 line → relay on). Add a pull-down on each input so nothing actuates while the ESP boots. The ESP grounds an 85 line to switch a relay; the relay only actually actuates when the box is powered (engine running), so firmware logic and hardware gate agree. v1 = on/off only; the MOSFET/PWM dimming of §06 is the v2 drop-in.

Flyback — handled by the ULN's internal clamps

The ULN2803A has built-in kickback (clamp) diodes for inductive loads. Tie its COM pin (10) to the +12 V rail (your commoned 86), and the internal diodes handle every coil's flyback — so you do not need to add the external 1N4007s on the box. (If you ever swap to discrete logic-level MOSFETs instead, then you'd add a 1N4007 per coil, band/cathode to the +12 side.)

Headlights stay outside the gated box — fail-ON

Lighting must survive an ESP or master-relay fault, so it is not on the master-gated box. Feed the headlight relays from a non-gated source and trigger their coils from the original headlight wire that runs through the start button — this relieves the starter switch of the bulb current and keeps the factory cranking cut. One bulb is always-on (ESP-independent, guaranteed); the second runs through a 5-pin NC (87a) relay the ESP energises only to shed it in a critical low-power moment. ESP dead → both bulbs lit; emergency shed → drop the second only. The ESP never has to provide light, only remove the one sheddable bulb.

PC817 opto — two isolated inputs

The 2-channel PC817 board is an input-isolation type (low output current — sensing, not coil driving). Use its two channels for the two 12 V status lines: ignition / engine-run sense and the start-button / headlight feed sense. Per channel: 12 V → ~1 kΩ → PC817 LED → ground; output collector → GPIO with a ~10 kΩ pull-up to 3.3 V. Logic inverts (12 V present pulls the GPIO low), so flip it in firmware. This gives transient-isolated inputs, replacing the divider-plus-Zener on the ignition line.

Sealed-box housekeeping

Because the ESP box is sealed: route the BLE antenna external (U.FL/IPEX) or against a plastic wall and clear of the metal subframe — the parked phone-sync depends on BLE actually reaching the garage. Plan OTA firmware updates (over BLE/WiFi) so you never open the box to reflash. Conformal-coat the board and add a small breather/desiccant against condensation. Keep the I²C bus short (< 15 cm), at 100 kHz, with ~4.7 kΩ pull-ups and local decoupling, routed away from the coil-drive wires; give the firmware an I²C bus-recovery routine + read timeouts so a glitch self-heals.

Future revision (noted, not now)

Consolidate the controller, sensors, coil drivers, and input protection onto a single custom PCB (fab e.g. JLCPCB) to replace the separate breakout boards — cleaner, more vibration-robust, and easier to seal. Design the relay coil-drivers, the always-hot buck + protection, the I²C sensors, and the ignition-sense front end as one board with proper connectors. Deferred until the v1 breadboard/protoboard build is proven.

22 Bench Verification Checklist

Do all of this on the bench with a variable supply before anything goes on the bike. It catches the expensive mistakes while they're still free.

01Trace the box. With the box powered and all relays off, confirm a5 (blue) reads 12 V (always-hot). Pull the 5 A fuse → if a5 dies, it's on the 5 A; if not, pull the 20 A. Note which fuse feeds which output.
02Set the buck. Adjust the MP1584 to 5.0 V and confirm it under load before connecting the ESP. Verify reverse-pol protection and TVS are in line.
03Flyback polarity. With a meter, confirm each coil diode is reverse-biased at rest (no continuity in the conducting direction across the energised rail). Energise one relay briefly and confirm it switches and releases cleanly.
04Engine-run logic. Simulate the states with the variable supply: 12.5 V no-ign = off; 12.5 V + ign = armed; 13.5 V + ign = relays enable; ign off = off-delay then drop; 13.8 V no-ign (charger case) = stays off.
05Load test each relay. Drive each coil from its ESP MOSFET and confirm the load switches, the ESP doesn't brown out, and there's no I²C disruption when relays switch (watch for sensor read errors).
06Sentinel + BLE. Confirm deep-sleep current is sane, motion/timer wakes fire, the queue persists across a power-cycle (NVS), and a paired phone can connect and drain it. Check BLE range with the lid closed.
07Only then mount on the bike, land grounds to the star, and bench-style re-test once before the fairings go back on.
Dev workflow — two units, one discipline

With a second ESP and buck on the bench, keep a standing rule:

  • Bench-twin first. The bench unit is a permanent regression rig, wired to the identical pinout/pull-ups as the bike unit and kept on the same firmware build. Every change is flashed and exercised there before it ever reaches the bike. Give each unit a device ID so logs tell them apart.
  • OTA with rollback, proven before sealing. Because the bike box is sealed, set up A/B OTA partitions with app-verification and automatic rollback from the very first flash (the partition table must include OTA slots before sealing). Prove on the bench that a deliberately-bad update rolls back cleanly — only then trust OTA on the sealed unit. A bricked sealed unit is a fairings-off afternoon.
  • Lock the buck pots. Set each MP1584 to 5.0 V under load independently (they vary unit-to-unit), then lock the trimpot with nail polish/thread-locker — vibration walks an unsecured pot over time, the bike unit especially.
  • Current-limited bench supply. Use an adjustable, current-limited PSU: the limit catches a wiring short before it cooks anything, and the variable voltage drives the engine-run state-machine tests (steps 04–05) entirely on the desk.
  • Stock cheap spares. Bucks, MOSFETs, and diodes are cents and ship slowly — keep a few so a dead part mid-build doesn't cost a weekend.

23 Charging System — R/R Choice & Stator Test

The charging upgrade that actually matters here is the regulator, not the stator. This section captures the R/R decision and — more importantly — how to measure whether you ever need to touch the stator at all.

R/R: series, and genuine

Go series, not MOSFET. A MOSFET R/R (Shindengen FH012AA/FH020AA) is still a shunt regulator — it runs the R/R cooler but leaves the stator at full output. A series R/R (Shindengen SH775 ≈ 35 A, or SH847 ≈ 50 A) disconnects the stator when charge isn't needed, cutting stator heat — the real protection on this generation. For a 400 W DL650 the SH775 is right-sized and easier to mount; the SH847 is the genuine OEM series unit Suzuki used on the 2014+ DL1000 (Suzuki PN 32800-31J00), which is the safest way to guarantee authenticity. Counterfeits matter — fake SH847s are shunt units in disguise, so buy genuine (Suzuki part number, Japanese-made, metal base plate, Shindengen logo). Wire it directly: three stator phases spliced + heatshrunk (ditch the OEM connectors), output to the battery on 10 AWG with a 30 A fuse, the sense lead (SH847) to battery +, good ground, battery disconnected during install. Healthy result: ~14.2–14.4 V at the battery, engine running.

Stator: measure, don't preemptively upgrade

For your loads (LED fog, grips, tablet, electronics) the stock 400 W stator plus a series R/R is almost certainly enough. Output is plentiful at cruise; it only drops at idle — so the real-world risk isn't melting anything, it's drawing the battery down while sitting in cold stop-and-go with everything on. That's exactly what your INA226 + load-shedding firmware manage (dim fogs / cut grips when the bus sags). So treat a stator upgrade as a "later, only if the data says so" item — and here's how to get that data.

How to test it

Charging-system health (do this first — multimeter at the battery):

A1Resting voltage — engine off, settled a few hours. Healthy 12.6–12.8 V; below ~12.4 V = low/old battery.
A2At idle, engine running. Should rise above resting; on the 650 it's modest at idle (~13–13.5 V is normal — output is low at idle).
A3At ~5000 rpm. Should climb to the regulated ~14.0–14.4 V. If it won't climb, the charging system is weak (stator or R/R).
A4Under full load at cruise rpm. All accessories on (fog, grips, tablet, headlight), ~4000–5000 rpm. Should hold ≥ 13.8 V. Sagging below ~13 V at cruise = genuinely exceeding capacity.
A5Log it with the INA226. Record bus voltage and total current at idle and cruise, worst case = idle with everything on. This quantifies your real draw vs. the ~30 A the stator can give, so the decision is data, not guesswork.

Stator-specific tests (to tell a bad stator from a bad R/R) — unplug the 3-phase stator connector first:

B1Phase resistance (engine off). Measure across each of the three phase pairs (1-2, 2-3, 1-3). Readings should be low and roughly equal (typically well under 1 Ω — check the service manual for the exact figure). A big imbalance or an open reading = bad winding.
B2Insulation to ground (engine off). From each phase to chassis/ground: should read open / infinite. Any continuity to ground = a shorted winding = stator failing.
B3AC output (engine running, stator still unplugged from R/R). On AC volts, measure each phase pair. Readings should be balanced across all three pairs and rise with rpm (the manual gives the exact V-AC-per-rpm spec). One pair low or zero = a dead phase. The leads are live AC — insulate them, don't short them, keep clear of hot/moving parts.
